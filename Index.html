From Classrooms to Communities:

A National Learning Immersion

Hi,

University outreach today faces a real challenge. While institutions offer strong academic programs, reaching students early enough with clarity, context, and credible exposure often remains fragmented. Career awareness arrives late. Choices feel overwhelming. And learning is frequently reduced to brochures, presentations, or one-time counselling sessions.

Outreach, for us, is about changing that.


It is the process of taking universities beyond their campuses and into schools and communities, meeting students where they are, long before application deadlines. Through structured workshops, career fairs, and hands-on simulations, outreach helps students understand emerging careers, academic pathways, and skill requirements in a way that feels real, not abstract.


The goal is simple: help students understand why learning matters, how careers actually work, and what possibilities exist before critical academic decisions are made.


At the center of this outreach was The Catalyst Project, Rishihood University’s experiential student engagement initiative designed to help learners explore career pathways, build real-world skills, and gain early clarity before making higher-education decisions. Through interactive workshops and hands-on learning, Catalyst brings university-level thinking directly into school classrooms.


The purpose of our method of outreach is to deliver the learning experience that the student will receive in the university directly into the classroom, directly to the students. Creating an image that learning is fun at this university, and then lead from there, this is the idea behind the outreach we did for Rishihood University. Here is a brief overview of what transpired.



Impact at a glance:

   

Our Strongholds:



Delhi NCR:

Delhi NCR became our core hub of activity.

We worked extensively with schools such as DAV Public School (Ashok Vihar & Pushpanjali Enclave), Tagore International School (Vasant Vihar & East of Kailash), KR Mangalam World School, The Maurya School (Gurugram), and Bal Bhavan Public School (Mayur Vihar).


Here we delivered sessions on:

• Careers of Tomorrow
• Design Thinking
• Liberal Arts Careers
• Financial Literacy
• Entrepreneur’s Fish Tank
• Career Counselling


Students explored Liberal Arts by unpacking common myths and mapping interdisciplinary career paths, from media and governance to business and design. They studied how figures like Steve Jobs, Oprah Winfrey, Howard Schultz, and Malala Yousafzai used curiosity and cross-disciplinary thinking to shape their impact.

In Design Thinking sessions, students interviewed peers, redesigned everyday objects, built empathy maps, and worked through the Double Diamond framework, Discover, Define, Develop, Deliver. They learned that design begins with listening.


Entrepreneur’s Fish Tank simulations transformed classrooms into startup labs. Students selected sectors (food, education, wellness, sustainability), identified real problems, built Business Model Canvases, defined customer segments, and pitched original ideas in 50–70 words. Teams debated pricing, partnerships, and feasibility. Quiet students stepped into leadership roles.


Career fairs across NCR created space for one-on-one counselling with students and parents navigating post-Grade 10 and 12 decisions, often for the first time in a structured way.


Madhya Pradesh: 

Following direct institutional demand, we expanded into Madhya Pradesh.

Workshops were conducted across Hamidia Boys School, CM Rise Sandipani Vidyalaya, Captain Varun Memorial H.S. School, Sarojini Naidu Higher Secondary School, and Imperial High Secondary School.


Across schools and coaching centres, students participated in:

• Fish Tank entrepreneurship simulations
• Career awareness workshops
• Financial literacy sessions


Bhopal and surrounding areas became a major focus.

Here, Fish Tank workshops and entrepreneurship sessions landed differently.

Classrooms had limited infrastructure but unlimited attention.


Students stayed back after sessions asking about funding, business viability, and whether someone from their background could realistically build something meaningful. Teachers helped translate concepts into local context. Many students were experiencing collaborative problem-solving for the first time.


These sessions reminded us that experiential learning is not enrichment for some, it is access for everybody.



Jammu

Across Jammu, we worked with schools including

Maharishi Vidya Mandir, RN Tagore Senior Secondary School, Carmel Convent School, SOS Hermann Gmeiner School,

and R.M. Public School.


Here, we delivered Careers of Tomorrow workshops focused on emerging fields, skill-based pathways, and decision-making frameworks.


Students explored how careers today are no longer linear, how technology, creativity, and entrepreneurship overlap. The conversations here centered on exposure: understanding what exists beyond conventional professions.


By the end of the Jammu leg, students were asking better questions, not “Which stream should I take?” but “What problems do I want to work on?”



Himachal Pradesh

In Palampur, we worked with

Crescent Senior Secondary School, Shivalik Radiance School, Gyan Jyoti Senior Secondary School, Chand Public School,

and Anuradha Public School.


Sessions like Use Your Brain created space for smaller groups and deeper conversations.

Students spoke openly about confidence, exam pressure, and self-belief. These workshops moved beyond careers into cognition, focus, and mindset.


Although Learning slowed down their Listening increased and these classrooms became reflection spaces.


Across India

Beyond these regions, our outreach extended to cities like Hyderabad, Kolkata, Agra, Varanasi, Noida, Ghaziabad, and Faridabad, working with institutions such as:


Meru International School (Hyderabad), Apeejay School (Kolkata), Holy Public School (Agra), Dalimss Sunbeam School (Varanasi),

and The Modern School (Faridabad).


Across these locations, we conducted focused workshops on financial literacy, leadership, entrepreneurship, new-age careers, and career counselling, often tailored to the immediate needs of the school or student group.


Some sessions were large-format career fairs. Others were small, high-engagement classrooms.

But the intent remained consistent: bring clarity, exposure, and practical frameworks directly to students where they are.


Overall Outcomes


By the end of this outreach:

• 8,000+ students experienced hands-on learning
• Schools embedded experiential workshops into academic calendars
• Parents received structured career guidance
• Students moved from passive listening to active participation


We’re grateful to Rishihood University and every partner school that welcomed us into their spaces.


This is only the beginning.
